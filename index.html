DevOps

  • Culture
  • Automation
  • Measurement
  • Sharing
Why am I telling you all this? Well as I've learned, it's important to understand the cultural background of where someone comes from. As we get into some definitions, I'm going to be speaking about DevOps in the CAMS model sense of the term: Culture, Automation, Measurement, and Sharing. And I'd say that it's a culture *of* automation, measurement, and sharing that makes it truly successful.

Enterprise

Software used to satisfy the needs of an organization rather than individual users
-Wikipedia
  • Payroll / HR systems
  • Classroom / lab computers
  • On-Campus LAN / Wi-Fi
When I say "enterprise," I'm going to be talking about organizations whose business is not software, or even IT, but whose IT department or division is focused on supporting the business operations of the organization as a whole. In my case, that means anything from the payroll and HR systems, to classroom and lab computers and printers, to the on-campus network.

Legacy

  • On-premises datacenters with physical servers
  • Days of downtime
  • Project-centric planning
  • Traditional org structure
When I talk about "legacy," I'm thinking of the "traditional" ways that IT organizations have operated: Things like on-prem datacenters and software, physical servers, or what I like to call "pre-scaled" hardware, upgrades that can take systems down for days at a time, projet-centric planning, and a traditional org structure.

Story Time

OK, so story time. All the way back in 2013, I was working at a small energy efficiency software company, doing some pretty cool stuff. In addition to being on the dev team, I was also "the" ops guy for most of my time there, and managed to get things like configuration management and automated deployments in place. Unfortunately, as is sometimes the case, I outgrew that small company and decided it was time to move up. So I looked around, and found something that really piqued my interest.

Opportunity

I saw an organization struggling to keep up with its users' expectations. Described as "backwater", with an Online Application for Graduate Admission that was, and I quote, "so bad, I almost didn't apply." And I saw the potential of being able to help make everything better. After all, I'd participated in and seen first-hand how things like configuration management and continuous delivery could do. As my start date approached, I got myself all amped up, right? "I'm gonna turn this ship around. I'm going in with guns blazing!"

Big Orgs Move Slow

https://channel9.msdn.com/Events/Build/2017/T6006-R1
So, not that we were *that* big, but big enough that it was a culture shock for me, coming from a smaller company prior. Continuing along the company size spectrum, Rob Wormald from Google's Angular team gave a talk at Microsoft Build a couple months back, and he mentioned how it took them two years to get TypeScript approved as a language that could be used company-wide. So if you're part of a big org, and things are moving slow, you're not alone, it's apparently just the nature of the beast.

Why?

  • Stability over innovation
  • Physical scaling limitations
  • Project-centric planning
But why though? Well, with legacy enterprise organizations, and by that I mean enterprise organizations who haven't really embraced "the new way" yet, it's because they value stability over innovation, and in some cases with good cause. How many of you would be upset if you didn't get your paycheck because the payroll system crashed? Obviously things like the State of DevOps Report and what DORA is doing are proving that you can do both, but it's hard, and so stability wins and innovation languishes. Similarly, when setting up a new system means budgeting for new hardware, you're limited in how fast you can roll things out. I also mentioned the project-centric nature of legacy organizations, and this is another contributor to the falling behind.

Software is not a Building

Don't construct them the same way

Projects, by definition, have a beginning and an end. Software, as many are still discovering, is constantly evolving. Yes, new software is created, and it eventually is sunset, but in that time it can grow tremendously, and as such, should not be constrained to the limitations of the physical world. While this may be easy for you to grasp, many still don't see it that way, so here's an example I like to use.
This is my home office. Things had to be done in a very specific order. For the ceiling alone, electical wiring, lights, insulation, resilient channel, A/V wiring (it's also a home theater) drywall, taping, primer, paint, crown molding. If I forgot a step and moved on, it would be difficult and expensive to remedy. And so you do the planning up-front to make sure you have all your ducks in a row. This is waterfall development, right? And in the days of shrinkwrapped software, that was how it had to be done, because once you shipped, there was no turning back. But we're not in that time anymore, and so we need our processes to adjust accordingly.

PL/SQL

  • Filesystem I/O
  • LDAP management
  • HTTP connections
Has anyone worked with PL/SQL before? So PL/SQL is what happens when Oracle thinks it's a good idea for your database to also handle all your application code. This includes everything from filesystem I/O, to LDAP management, to HTTP connections. The one thing it doesn't do, out of the box, is automated testing. So, what was one of the first things I wrote when I came on board? A lightweight automated testing library so that I, and the team could start writing tests against our code.

Be Prepared
For More Resistance
Than You Can Imagine

I get it. Right out of college, 2007, I didn't see the point of spending more time writing more code for something I could check quicker by hand. This is one of the most common arguments against automated testing, and for me at least, it wasn't until I saw that first failing test in unrelated code, that the value really hit me. The resistance doesn't usually come in the form of active refusal so much as apathy towards being forced to change long-lived habits. So how do you convince others? I like to lead by example. Share the knowledge. Show everyone the value first-hand. It will take a long time, but with patience, slowly others will come around.

Manifesto for Agile Software Development

We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it.

Through this work we have come to value:

  • Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
  • Working software over comprehensive documentation
  • Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
  • Responding to change over following a plan

That is, while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more.

This is the Manifesto for Agile Software Development. You may have seen it before. Everyone always focuses on the 4 bullet points, but I want to point out that first sentence. "We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it." They're talking about experimentation.

Small Controlled Experiments

http://verraes.net/2014/03/small-controlled-experiments/
This is an article by Mathias Verraes: Student of Systems, Meddler of Models, Laborer of Legacy. He's a Belgian developer I met at a conference just before this was published. In it, he goes over some steps for spending just a small amount of time each day experimenting and improving. It's along the same lines as Deming's PDCA cycle, or Toyota's Improvement Kata, but on a much smaller scale. Low-cost, low-risk, easy to implement or easy to revert. Kaizen, continuous improvement, should be at the heart of these experiments.
This is Footprints. It's your run-of-the-mill legacy/enterprise ticketing system. Look at all those fields! I count 24 on just this tab, and look at all those other tabs! This is where all our work was tracked. It took minutes to lookup, add, or edit a task, it had minimal visualization options, and had terrible cross-browser compatibility. So what did we do?
We tried an experiment. We kept our customer communication in Footprints, but switched to Trello for our day-to-day task management. The first step we tried was making a Kanban board in Footprints, but it was still too clunky and not configurable enough, so we went to Trello. It was a little bit to get used to, having to manage multiple tracking systems and keep certain information in sync, but the daily workflow improvements were well worth it. I heard someone in the peanut gallery just shout "show me the metrics!" Good thing I've got those! Weekly sprint planning, for example, we'd have about a dozen tasks. In Footprints, marking each of those as planned for the current sprint took 10 seconds. Trello, a 2 second drag-and-drop. So an 8 second difference times 12 tasks is a minute and a half. Not that much, but do that for a year, and you've wasted an hour of 3 or 4 people's time literally waiting for the browser to reload. Not to mention it breaking the flow of the weekly planning meeting.
Here's a picture of another one of our experiments: this is Go, the continuous delivery tool, running on a first-generation Intel Power Mac under my desk. This photo was taken in late 2014, which means the Mac was at least 4 years old, given when they were discontinued. Literally a spare machine, lying around, brought back to life to live again as a prototype CI server, because it was less effort than procuring a new "pre-scaled" server for the physical datacenter. We later found a Mac Mini that was more powerful, which was good because we continued adding more pipelines, and eventually, when we were able to show the value, we got a real server for it.

Decouple from your Enterprise Systems

"Now wait a minute," you might be saying, "you can't just willy-nilly deploy updates to an enterprise system!" And you're probably right. But we didn't do that. Remember the quote from the grad student who said that the online application was so bad they almost didn't apply? We rebuilt it, with Angular and Bootstrap and REST APIs, and more importantly with minimal hooks into the enterprise system.

Extend via Configuration, Don't Fork Vendor Code

Same with the private social network for prospective students. It was almost impossible to upgrade the underlying framework because of all the customizations that were built into it. We pulled out all that custom behavior and design into modules that could be plugged into the platform, and upgrades suddently became routine; boring, even. But that's the goal, right?

Look for Low-Hanging Fruit

Sometimes small changes can have a big impact. The self-service system, where students register for classes, check their grades and degree progress, even register for housing, was a specific source of pain for the campus.
So much so that one student created a Chrome extension to improve the user interface.
I ended up spending two days restyling the app to have a mobile-friendly responsive design. All this compiled into a single CSS file running on a separate server, and we just configure the app to use that instead of the original. Low-risk because the database code didn't need to get touched, high value because we knew that this was a big issue for students.

Bimodal IT

  • Mode 1: Slow and Steady
  • Mode 2: Fast and Agile

It doesn't need to be one or the other!

You can have your cake and eat it too!

It was around this time that the term "Bimodal IT" found its way to our organization. "Bimodal IT" comes from Gartner, and is the idea that in order to succeed in the new world of user expectations, your IT organization neds to operate two different ways in parallel. Mode 1 is for your traditional, predictable service requests, Mode 2 is for when there are more unknowns and iteration is necessary. This immediately raised two red flags for me. Number one: while this seems reasonable for a legacy organization, but for me, coming from the other direction, I was like "hold up, why isn't everything mode 2? If it's predictable, it should be automated, and then becomes an exploration of how best to automate." Number two is the false dichotomy that speed and stability are a trade-off. This is becoming clearer now, with the State of DevOps Report, etc. While Bimodal IT may be a good first step for those just getting started, it seemed like a step backwards from my past experience, and I tried to be vocal about that.

DevOps Strategy Team

  • Succinctly define DevOps
  • Give examples (milestones even!)
  • Show metrics
A series of mishaps led to the creation of a "DevOps Strategy Team," whose charge was to deliver a presentation to senior management about why it was worth investing in DevOps. Now, set aside the fact that we were already off on the wrong foot by having a "DevOps Team," I was at least glad to see management opening up to the idea of some fundamental change. Unfortunately, there was no clear direction, and everyone had a different idea of what DevOps meant and the problems is was supposed to solve. If I were to go back and do this again, I would make sure everyone is on the same page about what we're trying to accomplish, with a very succinct definition, examples even, and metrics to show how to tell if we're on the right track. We didn't have any of these, and so the whole initiative fell apart.

Time to shake things up!

The Re-Org

And then came the dreaded R word. It was clear to me, and everyone, that the organization needed some big changes. Unfortunately they were not what I was expecting. Or maybe deep down they were. The ops team got pulled further away from the dev team, and it became clear that the focus of the organization was shifting from service provider to service broker. This meant less of an emphasis on software development and more of an emphasis on off-the-shelf configuration and administration. Obviously not the thing a developer wants to hear.
And so, inevitably, one day, I gave up. When I looked back at all the time and effort that had gone into me trying to improve things, and what the relative outcome was, I realized something:

You (as an individual) Can't Change Culture

I couldn't change the culture.

You (as an individual) Can't Change Culture

  • Spark change in others
  • Lead by example (be the CAMS model!)
  • Be patient
I think it all comes back to this. I couldn't make the organization want to change any more than I could make our town's culture change. So what *can* you do? You can spark change in others, and I'd like to think I at least did that. You can lead by example, share your wins, pair with others to show them the benefits first-hand. And lastly, be patient. Did I just not stick around long enough? Maybe. Are they ever going to figure it out? Maybe.

Decouple, Decouple, Decouple

  • UI from Database
  • Front-End from Back-End
  • Custom Features from Vendor Software
The successes that we did have all seemed to stem from the idea of decoupling. Decouple the UI from the database so the code is more modular. Decouple the front-end from the back-end, and by this I mean the offices: the users completing their graduate application are using a completely different system from the admissions teams reviewing them. Decouple your custom features from the vendor software or platform they run on. Creating decoupled systems means smaller, less-risky changes, allowing for greater agility moving forward.
