Why am I telling you all this? Well, I've learned it's important to understand the cultural background of where someone comes from. It's also hard to discern without being a part of it. And indeed, culture is the first item in DevOps' CAMS model: Culture, Automation, Measurement, and Sharing. And it's a culture *of* automation, measurement, and sharing that makes it truly successful.

Big Orgs Move Slow

https://channel9.msdn.com/Events/Build/2017/T6006-R1
  • not *that* big, but still culture shock
  • not Google, but even Google has this problem
  • Rob Wormald, Angular, talk @ MSBuild

So, not that we were *that* big, but it was still a culture shock for me that things moved so slowly. Obviously we weren't Google, but even Google has this problem. Rob Wormald, from their Angular team, gave a talk at Microsoft Build a couple months back, and he mentioned how it took them two years to get TypeScript approved as a language that could be used company-wide. So if you're part of a big org, and things are moving slow, you're not alone, it's apparently just the nature of the beast.

Why?

  • Stability over innovation
  • Control change vs embrace it
  • Physical scaling limitations
  • Project-centric planning
  • but why though?
  • legacy enterprise orgs (define) value stability over innovation
  • one way = controlling change (rather than embracing)
  • payday example
  • research = can have both, but hard, so S wins + I languishes
  • budgeting + provisioning hardware limits rollout
  • not capital P project = doesn't have priority
But why though? Well, with legacy enterprise organizations, and by that I mean enterprise organizations who haven't really embraced "the new way" yet, it's because they value stability over innovation. One of the ways they do this is by controlling change, rather than embracing it. In some cases, that's with good cause, right? How many of you would be upset if you didn't get your paycheck because the payroll system crashed on payday after an upgrade? Research is starting to show that you can have both stability and innovation, but it's hard to get there, and so stability wins and innovation languishes. Similarly, when setting up a new system means budgeting for new hardware, you're limited in how fast you can roll new things out. I also mentioned the project-centric nature of legacy organizations, and this is another contributor: if it's not a capital P project, it doesn't have the priority.

Software is not a Building

Don't construct them the same way

  • projects by def have start and end
  • software, still discovering, organic, constantly evolving, like garden
  • shouldn't be constrained by same rules as construction
  • easy for some to grasp, many still don't see it
Projects, by definition, have a beginning and an end. Software, as many are still discovering, is organic, constantly evolving, like a garden, and as such, should not be constrained to the same rules as construction. While this may be easy for some to grasp, many still don't see it that way, so here's an example I like to use:
  • electical wiring, lights, insulation, resilient channel, A/V wiring, drywall, taping, primer, paint, crown molding
  • miss a step, difficult and expensive to remedy
  • planning up front to get ducks in row
  • waterfall development, shrinkwrapped software, physically shipped
  • not that time anymore, processes need to adjust
  • moved in, added floor, painted when time, no drywall dust?
  • how different process?
This is my home office. Things had to be done in a very specific order. For the ceiling alone, that meant electical wiring, followed by lights, then insulation, resilient channel, A/V wiring, drywall, taping, primer, paint, crown molding. If I forgot a step and moved on, it would be very difficult and expensive to remedy. And so you do the planning up-front to make sure you have all your ducks in a row and do things right the first time. This is waterfall software development, and in the days of shrinkwrapped software, that's how it was done, because the cost to ship -- physically ship -- was so high, and once you shipped, there was no turning back. But we're not in that time anymore, and so we need our processes to adjust accordingly. What if I could have moved in right away, been able to work, slid the floor in under my desk, painted the walls when I had time, not had to deal with any drywall dust? How different would my process have been?

PL/SQL

  • Filesystem I/O
  • LDAP management
  • HTTP connections
  • hadn't either till hired here
  • Oracle thinks DB should also handle all code
  • (fragment: examples)
  • no testing, so created lightweight library for me and team
Has anyone worked with PL/SQL before? I hadn't either, before being hired here. So PL/SQL is what happens when Oracle thinks it's a good idea for your database to also handle all your application code. This includes everything from filesystem I/O, to LDAP management, to HTTP connections. The one thing it doesn't do, out of the box, is automated testing. So, what was the first thing I wrote when I came on board? A lightweight automated testing library so that I, and the whole ERP team could start writing tests against our code.

Assume Awesomeness

https://github.com/BeardedCoder/RealWorldRetros
  • not malicious or defiant
  • assume awesomeness (define)
  • everyone is
  • Sean Yo, really stuck w/me
The first thing to remember that your colleagues probably aren't being purposely malicious or defiant. You need to "assume awesomeness" -- that, whatever their current situation is, they're trying their hardest to do the best job they can. Everyone on your team is. This is something I heard from a developer named Sean Yo, and it has really stuck with me through the years.

Lead By Example

"Be the CAMS model!"

  • not seeing succes w/advocacy, teaching or mentoring?

If you're not seeing a lot of success through advocacy, teaching or mentoring, try just leading by example. Exhibit what you'd like the culture to grow to. Automate all the things. Measure the gains you're making. Most imporantly, share your successes, and your failures. Be the CAMS model!

Small Controlled Experiments

http://verraes.net/2014/03/small-controlled-experiments/
  • Mathias Verraes, small amt of time every day experimenting and improving
  • Deming's PDCA cycle, Toyota's Improvement Kata, smaller scale
  • Low-cost, low-risk, easy to implement, easy to revert
  • Kaizen -- continuous improvement -- at the heart of them
Mathias Verraes wrote an article that discusses spending just a small amount of time each day experimenting and improving. It's along the same lines as Deming's PDCA cycle, or Toyota's Improvement Kata, but on a much smaller scale. Low-cost, low-risk, easy to implement or easy to revert. Kaizen, continuous improvement, should be at the heart of these experiments.

Decouple from your Enterprise Systems

  • remember grad app quote?
  • rebuilt it, Angular, Bootstrap, REST, minimal DB hooks
  • by decoupling, minimized risk, abstracted complexity, able to develop with agility
Remember the quote from the grad student who said that the online application was so bad they almost didn't apply? We rebuilt it, with Angular and Bootstrap and REST APIs, and most importantly, with minimal hooks into the enterprise system. By decoupling the application from the underlying database, we minimized risk, we abstracted away the complexity into a more modern API, and we were able to develop with agility.

Look for Low-Hanging Fruit

Sometimes small changes can have a big impact. The self-service system, where students register for classes, check their grades and degree progress, even select their housing, was a specific source of pain for the campus.
So much so that one student, a freshman at the time, Aaron Zufall, created a Chrome extension to improve the user interface. I thought this was great, though unfortunately a Chrome extension doesn't scale to 10,000 users running a dozen different browsers.
  • 2 days restyling to mobile-friendly, responsive design
  • compiled into single CSS file on separate server
  • configured app to use new stylesheet
  • low-risk, no code changes; low-cost, just a couple days; high-value, knew it was a big issue
So we ended up spending two days restyling the app to have a mobile-friendly responsive design. All this compiled into a single CSS file running on a separate server, and we just configured the app to use that instead of the original stylesheet. Low-risk because the database code didn't need to get touched, low-cost because it was just a couple days of Sass work, and high-value because we knew that this was a big issue for students.

Lead Times

  • ERP: 2-7 days
  • Web: 5-20 minutes
  • By doing all these things (list) + compound interest earned
  • achieved lead times orders of magnitude faster than ERP (over the wall)
  • Also able to deploy multiple times per day, compared to couple times a week
  • things looking good in web team bubble
By doing all of these things -- decoupling our apps, continuous integration, constant communication with ops -- and the compound interest we earned when combining them, we achieved lead times that were orders of magnitude faster than the process for ERP changes, which were historically more of the traditional "over-the-wall" approach. In doing so, we were also able to deploy multiple times per day, compared to a couple times a week. Things were looking pretty good in our little web team bubble!

  • despite victories, clear to me, everyone, org needed big changes
  • not what I was expecting

And then came the dreaded R word. Despite our victories on the web team, it was clear to me, and everyone I think, that the organization needed some big changes. Unfortunately they were not what I was expecting. The ops team got pulled further away from the dev team, and it became clear that the focus was shifting from software development to off-the-shelf solutions. Absolutely a valid direction for the department to take, just not what a developer like myself wants to hear.
And so, inevitably, one day, I gave up. When I looked back at all the time and effort that had gone into me trying to improve things, and what the relative outcome was, I realized something:

Decouple, Decouple, Decouple

  • UI from Database
  • Front-End from Back-End
  • Custom Features from Vendor Software
  • software successes stemmed from decoupling
  • UI from DB
  • front-end from back-office (grad app example)
  • custom from vendor
  • smaller, less-risky changes = greater agility
The software successes that we did have all seemed to stem from the idea of decoupling. Decouple the UI from the database so the code is more maintainable. Decouple the front-end from the back-end, and by this I mean the offices: the users completing their graduate application are on a completely different system from the admissions teams reviewing them. Decouple your custom features from the vendor software or platform they run on. Creating decoupled systems means smaller, less-risky changes, allowing for greater agility moving forward.
