Why am I telling you all this? Well, I've learned it's important to understand the cultural background of where someone comes from. Why?

  • Stability over innovation
  • Physical scaling limitations
  • Project-centric planning
  • but why though?
  • legacy enterprise orgs (define) value stability over innovation
  • payday example
  • research = can have both, but hard, so S wins + I languishes
  • budgeting + provisioning hardware limits rollout
  • not capital P project = doesn't have priority
But why though? Well, with legacy enterprise organizations, and by that I mean enterprise organizations who haven't really embraced "the new way" yet, it's because they value stability over innovation. In some cases, that's with good cause, right? How many of you would be upset if you didn't get your paycheck because the payroll system crashed on payday after an upgrade? Research is starting to show that you can have both stability and innovation, but it's hard to get there, and so stability wins and innovation languishes. Similarly, when setting up a new system means budgeting for new hardware, you're limited in how fast you can roll new things out. I also mentioned the project-centric nature of legacy organizations, and this is another contributor to the falling behind: if it's not a capital P project, it doesn't have the priority.

Software is not a Building

Don't construct them the same way

  • projects by def have start and end
  • software, still discovering, constantly evolving
  • yes, created, sunset, change tremendously
  • shouldn't be constrained by same rules
  • easy for some to grasp, many still don't see it
Projects, by definition, have a beginning and an end. Software, as many are still discovering, is constantly evolving. Yes, new software is created, and it eventually is sunset, but in that time it can change tremendously in that time, and as such, should not be constrained to the limitations of the physical world. While this may be easy for some to grasp, many still don't see it that way, so here's an example I like to use:
  • electical wiring, lights, insulation, resilient channel, A/V wiring, drywall, taping, primer, paint, crown molding
  • miss a step, difficult and expensive to remedy
  • planning up front to get ducks in row
  • waterfall development, shrinkwrapped software, physically shipped
  • not that time anymore, processes need to adjust
This is my home office. Things had to be done in a very specific order. For the ceiling alone, that meant electical wiring, followed by lights, then insulation, resilient channel, A/V wiring, drywall, taping, primer, paint, crown molding. If I forgot a step and moved on, it would be very difficult and expensive to remedy. And so you do the planning up-front to make sure you have all your ducks in a row and do things right the first time. This is waterfall development, right? And in the days of shrinkwrapped software, that's how it had to be done, because once you physically shipped, there was no turning back. But we're not in that time anymore, and so we need our processes to adjust accordingly.

PL/SQL

  • Filesystem I/O
  • LDAP management
  • HTTP connections
  • hadn't either till hired as EAD on ERP team
  • Oracle thinks DB should also handle all code
  • (fragment: examples)
  • no testing, so created lightweight library for me and team
Has anyone worked with PL/SQL before? I hadn't either, before being hired as an Enterprise Application Developer for the Enterpise Resource Planning (or ERP) team. So PL/SQL is what happens when Oracle thinks it's a good idea for your database to also handle all your application code. This includes everything from filesystem I/O, to LDAP management, to HTTP connections. The one thing it doesn't do, out of the box, is automated testing. So, what was the first thing I wrote when I came on board? A lightweight automated testing library so that I, and the whole ERP team could start writing tests against our code.

I get it. Right out of college, 2007, I didn't see the point of spending more time writing more code for something I could check quicker by hand. This is one of the most common arguments against automated testing, and for me at least, it wasn't until I saw that first failing test in unrelated code, that the value really hit me. Now, this resistance doesn't usually come in the form of active refusal so much as it does apathy towards being forced to change long-lived habits. So how do you convince others?

The first thing to remember that your colleagues probably aren't being purposely malicious or defiant. You need to "assume awesomeness" -- that, whatever their current situation is, they're trying their hardest to do the best job they can. Everyone on your team is. This is something I heard from a presentation by a Canadian developer named Sean Yo, and it has really stuck with me through the years.

Small Controlled Experiments

http://verraes.net/2014/03/small-controlled-experiments/
  • Mathias Verraes, small amt of time every day experimenting and improving
  • Deming's PDCA cycle, Toyota's Improvement Kata, smaller scale
  • Low-cost, low-risk, easy to implement, easy to revert
  • Kaizen -- continuous improvement -- at the heart of them
Here's an article by Mathias Verraes, a Belgian developer I met at a conference just before this was published. In it, he talks about spending just a small amount of time each day experimenting and improving. It's along the same lines as Deming's PDCA cycle, or Toyota's Improvement Kata, but on a much smaller scale. Low-cost, low-risk, easy to implement or easy to revert. Kaizen, continuous improvement, should be at the heart of these experiments.

Lead Times

  • Web: 5-20 minutes
  • By doing all these things (3) + compound interest earned, achieved lead times orders of magnitude smaller than ERP (over the wall)

By doing all of these things -- automated testing, continuous integration, constant communication with ops -- and the compound interest we earn when combining them, we achieved lead times that were orders of magnitude smaller than the process for ERP changes, which were historically more of the traditional "over-the-wall" approach. In doing so, we were also able to deploy multiple times per day, compared to the couple times a week others experienced. This all started out as experiments.
"Now wait a minute, Mr. Socks Fox" you might be saying, "you can't just willy-nilly deploy updates to an enterprise system over Slack!" And you're probably right. But we didn't do that.

Bimodal IT

  • Mode 1: Slow and Steady
  • Mode 2: Fast and Agile

It doesn't need to be one or the other!

You can have your cake and eat it too!

  • comes from Gartner, in order to succeed, need to work in 2 modes in parallel
  • Mode 1: traditional, predictable requests
  • Mode 2: those requiring exploration and iteration
  • may seem reasonable for legacy organization, rose 2 red flags
  • #1, where I was coming from, if predictable, why not automate (and then it becomes exploration)
  • (fragment) #2, false dichotomy of speed/stability trade-off (again, it's not, you can do both)
  • may be a good first step, seemed like a step backwards for me, tried to share
It was around this time that the term "Bimodal IT" found its way to our organization. "Bimodal IT" comes from Gartner, and is the idea that in order to succeed in the new world of user expectations, your IT organization neds to operate two different ways in parallel. Mode 1 is for your traditional, predictable service requests, Mode 2 is for when there are more unknowns and iteration is necessary. This immediately raised two red flags for me. Number one: while this seems reasonable for a legacy organization, for me, coming from the other direction, I was like "hold up, why isn't everything Mode 2? If it's predictable, it should be automated, and then becomes an exploration of how best to automate." Number two is the false dichotomy that speed and stability are a trade-off. Again, research is showing that it's not: you can do both. It's hard, but you can have both speed and stability. So while Bimodal IT may be a good first step for those just getting started, it seemed like a step backwards from my past experience, and I tried to be share my point of view about that, and a lot of other ideas as well.

Decouple, Decouple, Decouple

  • UI from Database
  • Front-End from Back-End
  • Custom Features from Vendor Software
  • software successes stemmed from decoupling
  • UI from DB
  • front-end from back-office (grad app example)
  • custom from vendor
  • smaller, less-risky changes = greater agility
The software successes that we did have all seemed to stem from the idea of decoupling. Decouple the UI from the database so the code is more modular. Decouple the front-end from the back-end, and by this I mean the offices: the users completing their graduate application are using a completely different system from the admissions teams reviewing them. Decouple your custom features from the vendor software or platform they run on. Creating decoupled systems means smaller, less-risky changes, allowing for greater agility moving forward.
