DevOps

  • Culture
  • Automation
  • Measurement
  • Sharing
So why am I telling you all this? Well as I've learned, it's important to understand the cultural background of where someone comes from. As we get into some definitions, I'm going to be speaking about DevOps in the CAMS model sense of the term: Culture, Automation, Measurement, and Sharing. And I'd say that it's a culture *of* automation, measurement, and sharing that makes it truly successful.

Enterprise

Software used to satisfy the needs of an organization rather than individual users
-Wikipedia
  • Payroll / HR systems
  • Classroom / lab computers
  • On-Campus LAN / Wi-Fi
When I say "enterprise," I'm going to be talking about organizations whose business is not software, or even IT, but whose IT department or division is focused on supporting the business operations of the organization as a whole. In my case, that means anything from the payroll and HR systems, to classroom and lab computers and printers, to the on-campus network.

Legacy

  • On-premises datacenters
  • Physical servers
  • Project-centric planning
  • Traditional org structure
When I talk about "legacy," I'm thinking of the "traditional" ways that IT organizations have operated: Things like on-prem datacenters and software, physical servers, or what I like to call "pre-scaled" hardware, projet-centric planning, and a traditional org structure.

Story Time

OK, so story time. All the way back in 2013, I was working at a small energy efficiency software company, doing some pretty cool stuff. In addition to being on the dev team, I was also "the" ops guy for most of my time there, and managed to get things like configuration management and automated deployments in place. Unfortunately, as is sometimes the case, I outgrew that small company and decided it was time to move up. So I looked around, and found something that really piqued my interest.

Opportunity

I saw an organization struggling to keep up with its users' expectations. Described as "backwater", with an Online Application for Graduate Admission that was, and I quote, "so bad, I almost didn't apply." And I saw the potential of being able to help make everything better. After all, I'd participated in and seen first-hand how things like configuration management and continuous delivery could do. As my start date approached, I got myself all amped up, right? "I'm gonna turn this ship around. I'm going in with guns blazing!"

PL/SQL

  • Filesystem I/O
  • LDAP management
  • HTTP connections
Has anyone worked with PL/SQL before? So PL/SQL is what happens when Oracle thinks it's a good idea for your database to also handle all your application logic. This includes everything from filesystem I/O, to LDAP management, to HTTP connections. The one thing it doesn't do, out of the box, is automated testing. So, what was one of the first things I wrote when I came on board? A lightweight automated testing library so that the team could start writing tests against their code.

Manifesto for Agile Software Development

We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it.

Through this work we have come to value:

  • Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
  • Working software over comprehensive documentation
  • Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
  • Responding to change over following a plan

That is, while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more.

This is the Manifesto for Agile Software Development. You may have seen it before. Everyone always focuses on the 4 bullet points, but I want to point out that first sentence. "We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it." They're talking about experimentation.
