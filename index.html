Good afternoon! I'm Steve, and I'm here to tell you a story about how I promoted DevOps in a legacy organization, legacy meaning the traditional, over-the-wall interactions between dev and ops.

Story Time

Many years ago, after doing some cool DevOps-y stuff at a small SaaS company, it was time to look for a new job. One position in particular cauight my eye, and I saw something:

Opportunity

I saw an organization struggling to keep up with expectations. And I saw the potential of using my first-hand DevOps experience make everything better. I was all like "Yeah! I'm gonna turn this ship around! I'm going in with guns blazing!"

Do Not Go In
With Guns Blazing

But there's a reason things are the way they are, and it's probably not by accident: legacy enterprise organizations value stability over innovation.
  • Stability over Innovation
  • Control change vs embrace it
One way they do this is by controlling change, rather than embracing it. In some cases, that's with good cause, because I know how many of you would be upset if you didn't get your paycheck due to a bad patch.

Lead By Example

"Be the CAMS model!"

But we're learning that you can do both, and that DevOps culture is the way to do that. Exhibit what you'd like the culture to grow to. Automate all the things. Measure the gains you're making. Most imporantly, share your successes, and your failures. Be the CAMS model!

Manifesto for Agile Software Development

We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it.

Through this work we have come to value:

  • Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
  • Working software over comprehensive documentation
  • Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
  • Responding to change over following a plan

That is, while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more.

This is the Manifesto for Agile Software Development. Everyone always focuses on the 4 bullet points, but I want to point out that first sentence. "We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it." They're talking about experimentation.
This is Go the CD tool, running on a spare machine under my desk, brought back to life as a prototype CI server, because it was less effort than procuring a new server for the physical datacenter. Once we were able to show its value, we got a real server for it.
Footprints is your run-of-the-mill legacy/enterprise ticketing system, where all our work was tracked. Just filling out the form to get something deployed was a whole ordeal in itself. So what did we do?
ChatOps, of course! I ask Todd, our ops guy, to update an app to a given tag, and it gets deployed. What you don't see is all the automated behind-the-scenes stuff, from tests running to notifications for auditing. All this is sowing the seeds for continuous delivery.
"Now wait a minute, Mr. Socks Fox" you might be saying, "you can't just willy-nilly deploy updates to an enterprise system over Slack!" And you're probably right. But we didn't do that.

Decouple from your Enterprise Systems

We started rebuilding apps, with Angular and Bootstrap and REST APIs, and most importantly, with minimal hooks into the enterprise systems. By decoupling the application from the underlying database, we minimized risk and reduced complexity.

Extend via Configuration,
Don't Fork Vendor Code

It was almost impossible to upgrade one of our apps because of all the customizations added to the underlying framework. We pulled all that out into configurable modules that could be plugged into the platform, and upgrades suddently became a breeze.

Look for Low-Hanging Fruit

Sometimes small changes can have a big impact. The self-service system, where students register for classes, check their grades and degree progress, even select their housing, was a specific source of pain for the campus.
So much so that one student, a freshman at the time, Aaron Zufall, created a Chrome extension to improve the UI. I thought this was great, though unfortunately a Chrome extension doesn't scale to 10,000 users running a dozen different browsers.
So I spent two days restyling the app to have a mobile-friendly responsive design. The CSS lived on a separate server, so this was low-risk because the database code didn't need to get touched, low-cost because it was just a couple days, and high-value because we knew that this was a big issue for students.

Lead Times

  • ERP: 2-7 days
  • Web: 5-20 minutes
By doing all of these things -- decoupling our apps, continuous integration, constant communication with ops -- we achieved crazy fast lead times compared to the traditional "over-the-wall" approach, which also meant we could deploy multiple times per day, compared to just a couple times a week.
  1. Lead by example (be the CAMS model!)
  2. Experiment
  3. Decouple, Decouple, Decouple
A quick review of things you can do to promote DevOps at your legacy org: exhibit the culture you want to see evolve; experiment, even if that means needing to do it under the radar; and decouple as much as you can to make changes easier and less risky.
